Brute Force
Attack Types

Understand how attackers crack passwords — from simple brute force to advanced hybrid attacks. Select an attack type to simulate it.

⚡
Brute Force
Extreme speed

Tries every possible combination of characters systematically. Guaranteed to find the password eventually — but time scales exponentially with length.

📖
Dictionary
High speed

Uses a precompiled wordlist of common passwords, phrases, and leaked credentials. Extremely effective — 80%+ of users choose predictable passwords.

🔀
Hybrid
High speed

Combines dictionary words with brute force mutations — appending numbers, capitalising letters, substituting characters. Catches "Password123!" style passwords.

🌈
Rainbow Table
Near-instant

Pre-computed hash lookup tables. Trades storage space for speed — cracks unsalted MD5/SHA1 hashes in seconds. Defeated entirely by salting.

🗂
Credential Stuffing
Medium speed

Uses username/password pairs from data breaches against other services. Effective because ~65% of people reuse passwords across sites.

💨
Password Spraying
Slow (by design)

Tests a few common passwords against many accounts. Bypasses account lockouts by staying below thresholds — a favourite for corporate network intrusion.

// your defence

How to defend against each attack

Against brute force & dictionary

  • Use passwords 16+ characters long
  • Mix uppercase, lowercase, numbers & symbols
  • Use a passphrase: "Purple$Horse!Sings7"
  • Enable account lockout after N failed attempts

Against rainbow tables

  • Always salt hashes with a random per-user value
  • Use bcrypt, scrypt, or Argon2 — never MD5/SHA1
  • Increase work factor as hardware improves

Against stuffing & spraying

  • Never reuse passwords — use a password manager
  • Enable MFA on every account
  • Monitor logins with anomaly detection
  • Check HaveIBeenPwned.com regularly

Social Engineering
Attack Types

Phishing comes in many forms. Learn to identify each type, recognise the red flags, and counter them before they succeed.

📧

Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonating trusted brands (banks, PayPal, Microsoft) to steal credentials or install malware via malicious links or attachments.

Critical
▸ Red flags
  • Sender domain doesn't match brand (paypa1.net vs paypal.com)
  • Urgent language: "act in 24 hours or your account closes"
  • Generic greeting ("Dear Valued Customer")
  • Hover shows different URL than link text
✓ Countermeasures
  • Always hover links before clicking — check the real destination
  • Go directly to the site by typing the URL yourself
  • Enable spam filters and report suspicious emails
🎯

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks using personal information gathered from LinkedIn, social media, or prior breaches to craft convincing, personalised emails.

Critical
▸ Red flags
  • Email uses your real name, employer, or recent activity
  • Appears to come from a known colleague with slightly wrong domain
  • Requests unusual data or urgent wire transfers
✓ Countermeasures
  • Verify unusual requests via phone — not by reply email
  • Limit personal info on public social profiles
  • Implement DMARC, DKIM, SPF
📱

Smishing (SMS)

Text messages crafted to create urgency — fake bank alerts, parcel notifications, or competition wins. Mobile users are more likely to click without scrutiny.

High
▸ Red flags
  • Unsolicited message from an unknown number
  • Shortened URLs hiding the real destination
  • "Your parcel is on hold — pay €1.99" style hooks
✓ Countermeasures
  • Never click SMS links — go to the official app or website directly
  • Your bank will never text asking for credentials
  • Report smishing to your carrier (forward to 7726)
🎙

Vishing (Voice)

Phone calls impersonating IT support, government agencies, or financial institutions. AI voice cloning makes this even more convincing.

High
▸ Red flags
  • Caller creates panic ("your account is being hacked right now")
  • Requests remote access via AnyDesk/TeamViewer
  • Asks for gift cards or cryptocurrency
✓ Countermeasures
  • Hang up and call back on the official number
  • Never grant remote desktop access to an unsolicited caller
🐋

Whaling

Spear phishing targeting C-suite executives (CEO, CFO) to authorise large fraudulent wire transfers or expose sensitive corporate information.

High
▸ Red flags
  • Impersonates CEO requesting urgent confidential transfer
  • "Don't tell anyone — board-level instruction"
  • Uses executive's name but wrong domain
✓ Countermeasures
  • Dual-authorisation for all wire transfers over a threshold
  • Verbal confirmation required for unusual financial requests
🔗

Pharming & DNS Spoofing

Redirects users to fraudulent websites even when they type the correct URL, by corrupting DNS records or the user's hosts file.

Critical
▸ Red flags
  • SSL certificate doesn't match the domain
  • Browser shows "Not Secure" on known HTTPS sites
  • Login fails even with correct credentials
✓ Countermeasures
  • Always check the padlock and domain in the address bar
  • Use DNSSEC resolvers (Cloudflare 1.1.1.1)

Spot Every Red Flag

A real phishing email, fully annotated.

📬 Inbox · 1 unread⚠ Suspicious sender
Fromsecurity@paypa1-support.net
Toyou@company.com
Subject⚠ Urgent: Your account has been temporarily suspended
DateToday, 02:17 AM
Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your PayPal account. To avoid permanent suspension, you must verify your identity within 24 hours.

→ Verify Your Account Now

If you do not act, your account will be permanently disabled.

Regards,
PayPal Security Team
#1 Spoofed domain — "paypa1" uses digit 1 instead of letter l. Not paypal.com.
#2 Sent at 2:17 AM from a .net address — real PayPal emails come from @paypal.com.
#3 Extreme urgency — "24 hours", "permanently disabled" prevents rational thinking.
#4 Link leads to a .ru domain with a suspicious token — hover before you click.
#5 Generic "Dear Valued Customer" — your real bank knows your full name.

Security
Best Practices

Concrete, actionable steps to strengthen your personal and organisational security posture — starting today.

01

Use a Password Manager

Never reuse passwords. Tools like Bitwarden or 1Password generate and store unique credentials for every site — one master password secures them all.

02

Enable MFA Everywhere

Add a TOTP app (Authy, Aegis) or hardware key (YubiKey) to every critical account. Even if your password leaks, attackers can't log in without the second factor.

03

Hover Before You Click

Always preview the real URL destination before clicking any link in email, SMS, or chat. Navigate directly to sites by typing the URL rather than following links.

04

Keep Software Updated

Enable automatic updates for OS, browsers, and plugins. Most exploits target known vulnerabilities — outdated software is low-hanging fruit for attackers.

05

Use 16+ Character Passwords

Length is the single most effective password defence. A 16-character random password takes centuries to brute-force even with modern GPU clusters.

06

VPN on Public Wi-Fi

Public hotspots are prime interception targets. A reputable VPN encrypts your traffic, preventing man-in-the-middle attacks at cafés, airports, and hotels.

07

3-2-1 Backup Rule

Keep 3 copies of data on 2 different media with 1 offsite or cloud backup. This is your primary defence against ransomware and hardware failure.

08

Principle of Least Privilege

Grant users and apps only the permissions they strictly need. Use a standard account day-to-day and reserve admin rights for actual administrative tasks.

09

Monitor for Breaches

Register at HaveIBeenPwned.com to get alerts when your email or passwords appear in known breach databases — then change affected credentials immediately.

10

Use Strong Hashing

Developers: always hash passwords with bcrypt, scrypt, or Argon2 with a unique per-user salt. Never MD5 or SHA1 — rainbow tables crack these in seconds.

11

Verify Unusual Requests

For unexpected requests involving money, access, or sensitive data — always call back on the official number. Social engineers exploit urgency and authority.

12

Security Awareness Training

Run regular phishing simulations and training sessions. Human error causes 90%+ of breaches. Educated employees are the most effective security control.
