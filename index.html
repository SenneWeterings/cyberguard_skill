Social Engineering Threats

Learn to identify phishing, smishing, vishing, and other social engineering attacks before they trick you.

📧

Email Phishing

Fraudulent emails impersonating trusted organisations to steal credentials or install malware.

Critical
📱

Smishing (SMS)

Text messages crafted to create urgency — fake bank alerts, parcel tracking, or prize notifications.

High
🎙

Vishing (Voice)

Phone call scams impersonating IT support, government agencies, or financial institutions.

High
🐋

Whaling

Highly targeted spear-phishing aimed at executives, CFOs, and C-suite employees for large wire transfers.

Medium
🔗

Pharming

DNS manipulation that redirects users to fraudulent websites even when they type the correct URL.

Critical
🧑‍💻

Pretexting

Fabricated scenarios used to establish false trust — posing as HR, IT, or contractors to extract data.

Medium

Spot the Phishing Email

Study this simulated phishing email and identify the red flags below.

📬 Inbox · 1 unread ⚠ Suspicious sender detected
From: [email protected]
To: [email protected]
Subject: ⚠ Urgent: Your account has been temporarily suspended
Date: Today, 02:17 AM
Dear Valued Customer, We have detected unusual activity on your PayPal account. To avoid permanent suspension, you must verify your identity within 24 hours. Click the link below to restore access immediately: → http://www.paypa1-secure-verify.ru/login?token=a93kd If you do not act, your account will be permanently disabled and any pending funds will be held. Regards, PayPal Security Team
🚩 Red flags identified
#1 Spoofed domain — "paypa1" uses a number "1" instead of the letter "l" to mimic PayPal.
#2 Sent at 2 AM from a .net address, not paypal.com — legitimate companies use their own domain.
#3 Urgency and fear tactics — "24 hours", "permanently disabled" to prevent rational thinking.
#4 Suspicious link with a .ru TLD and random token parameter — hover before you click.
#5 Generic greeting "Dear Valued Customer" — your real bank knows your name.

Security Best Practices

Concrete, actionable steps to strengthen your personal and organisational security posture.

01

Use a Password Manager

Never reuse passwords. A manager like Bitwarden or 1Password generates and stores unique credentials for every site — one master password secures them all.

02

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Add a TOTP app (Authy, Google Authenticator) or hardware key (YubiKey) to every critical account. Even if your password leaks, attackers can't log in.

03

Verify Before You Click

Hover over links to preview the real URL. When in doubt, navigate directly to the website rather than following email links. Report suspicious messages to your IT team.

04

Keep Software Updated

Enable automatic updates for OS, browser, and plugins. Most exploits target known vulnerabilities that patches already fixed — outdated software is low-hanging fruit for attackers.

05

Use a VPN on Public Wi-Fi

Public hotspots are prime interception targets. A reputable VPN encrypts your traffic, preventing man-in-the-middle attacks on cafés, airports, and hotel networks.

06

Back Up with the 3-2-1 Rule

Keep 3 copies of data, on 2 different media, with 1 offsite or cloud backup. This is your primary defence against ransomware and hardware failure.

07

Principle of Least Privilege

Grant users and applications only the permissions they strictly need. Admin accounts should only be used for administrative tasks — use a standard account day-to-day.

08

Monitor for Data Breaches

Register at HaveIBeenPwned.com to receive alerts if your email or passwords appear in known breach databases — then change affected credentials immediately.
